Take a Look Over the Past Years

This visualization displays anomalies from the average mean temperatures from 1880 to 2016 in degrees Celsius. Positive temperature anomalies, which depart from reference values or long-time averages, indicate that the observed temperature was warmer than the reference value. This temperature trend is influenced by greenhouse gas emissions, as carbon dioxide emissions are now more abundant in the earth’s atmosphere than any time in the last 800,000 years. Data for this visualization is from the GISS Surface Temperature (GISTEMP) analysis and the global component of Climate at a Glance (GCAG).

Carbon Dioxide Emissions

This visualization illustrates the USA carbon emissions found in the atmosphere created by fossil fuels from 1751 to 2014. The evident rise in emissions is tracked in million metric tons of carbon. Data for this visualization comes from the Carbon Dioxide Information Analysis Center (CDIAC).

source

This visualization tracks the annual growth of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere from 1959 to 2017. An upwards trend can be visually recognized with this bar graph, which tracks the rise in CO2 mole fraction increase (ppm). Data for this visualization was sourced from the US Government’s Earth System Research Laboratory, Global Monitoring Division. Two main series are provided: the Mauna Loa series (which has the longest continuous series since 1958) and a Global Average series (a global average over marine surface sites).
